Military, Health and Education Expenditure of the G20
By Sandra Valdés Salas

Introduction

In 2010, the GDP of emerging economies of the G20 represented 33% of the total GDP of all G20 countries. By the end of the decade, in 2018, emerging economies contributed with 39% of the total GDP of the G20 (which represented 66.5 trillion USD). China's economic growth was responsible for most of this change: in 2018, China's GDP mounted to 13.6 trillion USD. Nonetheless, other emerging economies have grew abruptly between 2010 and 2018, as is the case of India, South Korea (with an economic growth of 49% and 35% respectively), as well as Saudi Arabia and Indonesia (with an economic growth of 17%).

Overall, the economic breach between emerging and developed economies showed a reduction during the 2010s. For this reason, we will take a look at the major trends of the G20 economies with regards to expenditure on military, health, and education between 2010 and 2017. Our aim is not only to identify the main differences between emerging and developed economies, but also to understand which can be the main drivers of growth during this decade.

Military Expenditure

Average Military Spending of the G20 (2010-2017)

The United States has the largest military expenditure. Over an 8-year period (2010-2017), the U.S. spent, on average, 643 billion USD, followed by China (which have spent, for the same period, 181 billion USD on military sector), Russia (with 73.2 billion USD), and Saudi Arabia (with 64.9 billion USD).

Although the U.S. military expenditure is still, by far, the highest, the gap between the U.S. and other emerging economies has been getting smaller with regards to military expenditure. For instance, on 2010, the american country spent 6 times more than China on the military sector. But, in 2017, the U.S. only spent 2.7 times more than the chinese country (see below).


Absolute numbers can be deceiving...

...let´s take a look at relative values.


When compared to each country's GDP, Saudi Arabia has the largest military spending. And it´s increasing! Between 2010 and 2017, Saudi Arabia's military expenditure as a percentage of GDP grew 1.7 percentage points. Conversely, over the same 8-year period, the U.S decreased its military expenditure as a percentage of GDP by almost the same proportion (-1.6 points).

During the 2010s, emerging economies (in green) have spent less in military resources per person than developed economies (in gray) with few exceptions. Saudi Arabia became the country with the largest military per capita expenditure after 2012, surpassing the U.S. Of course, with a population of only 33.7 million people, Saudis can easily increase their per capita expenditure, while the U.S. might struggle with its population of 326 million people.

China remains among the countries of the bottom. With a population of more than 1,000 million people, is not surprising that China's military spending has been less the 200 USD per person.

Health Expenditure

Let´s take a look at trends on health.

When it comes to health expenditure, the U.S. is once again the country with the largest figure in absolute terms. Overall, G20 countries spend more on health than on military resources with the clear exception of Saudi Arabia that spends more on the military sector.

There is a clear division between emerging and developed economies of the G20 when it comes to health spending during the 2010s: all developed economies tend to allocate a higher percentage of GDP on health than the emerging economies. This has not only been a constant during the 2010s. But also, there is a clear division when it comes to per capita spending (see below).

In terms of per capita spending on health, emerging economies (in green) have remained in the bottom during the 2010s, whereas developed economies (in gray) have remained above, with the U.S. floating far on the top.

The good news...

most G20 economies have increased their spending on health.

Saudi Arabia and economies in Asia-Pacific increased their spending on health as a share of their GDP. Tragically, Mexico and Turkey have decided to go in the opposite direction.

Public or private spending?

Let´s take a look at private and public spending trends and its effects on health.

There is no rule set on stone when it comes to public and private spending per person among the G20 during the 2010s. On the one hand, more than 3/4 of health expenditure per person in Japan and Europe comes from public resources. On the other hand, more than half of health expenditure per person in India, Indonesia, Brazil and the U.S. comes from the private sector. What would be better for the population?

We can see a slight correlation between life expectancy and per person public spending on health. In those countries where the government spent, on average between 2010 and 2017), more on health per person than the private sector, life expectancy is higher. Take for instance the top 5 countries with the largest share of per capita health expenditure financed by the public sector (in yellow) versus the top 5 countries with the largest share of per capita health expenditure financed by the private sector (in green).

Education Expenditure

Finally, let's talk about education.

Note about the data: Most of the data includes indicators from 2010 to 2016. The missing figures on 2017 were calculated based on the average of the two previous years. Please take a look at my Methodology Notes at the bottom of this page.
Note: Saudi Arabia's indicators on education are based on the latest year available, 2006.

Overall, G20 countries have spent, on average, more on education than the military sector from 2010 to 2017, with few exceptions like Russia. The rest of the economies have spent: a) a lot more on education (with a difference of more than a 100 USD billions), b) more on education (with a difference of more than 50 USD billions but less than 100), or c) similar figures with a difference of less than a 50 USD billions). Let's take a closer look at differences among developed and emerging economies (see below).


When analyzing education expenditure as a percentage of GDP, surprisingly, the top countries with the largest figures are emerging economies: South Africa and Brazil. However, with an approximate GDP per capita of 6,000 and 11,000 USD respectively, education expenditure of the these countries becomes irrelevant (see below).

Education spending per person follows a similar pattern to what we saw with health: emerging economies (in green) appear at the bottom, whereas developed economies (in gray) tend to spend more on education per person. As opposed to the military and health sectors, Australia champions on education expenditure per person.

The bad news...

education has not been a priority for most of the G20 countries.

Overall, education expenditure as a percentage of GDP has decreased among the G20 countries. However, on a more positive tone, most emerging economies (with the exception of China, Mexico, and Russia) have allocated a larger percentage of their GDP to the education sector.

To sum up...


The Facts

Overall, the U.S. spends a lot of money on the military sector, but Saudi Arabia spends the most on the military resources per person. The U.S. is the undisputable champion on health: the american country spends the most in absolute terms, per person, and as a percentage of GDP. Intersetingly, public spending on health seems to matter. Finally, Australia is the leader when it comes to spending on education per person.

The Bad News

Some emerging countries (China, Russia, Saudi Arabia) are getting closer to the U.S. in terms of overall military spending. The worse is that education has not been priority for most of the countries of the G20.

The Good News

The U.S. has decreased its military expenditure as a percentage of GDP between 2010 and 2017. During this decade, most countries increased their percentage of GDP allocated to health expenditure.

The fastest growing economies among the emerging countries (Saudi Arabia, China, and South Korea), increased their expenditure on health as a percentage of GDP in more than 20% during the decade. This tells us about the importance of health on economic growth for emerging economies of the G20.

About education indicators:

Education figures were obtained, mainly, from the World Bank Open Data website. Most of the figures included data from 2010 to 2016 for the majority of the countries. The WB data does not include the following countries: Canada, France, Japan, Turkey, the Republic of Korea, China, and Saudi Arabia. The data for those countries was obtained from the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) Open Data website. The data from China was obtained from government statistics. Saudi Arabia is the only country with no available data after 2010.

Once all the data was retrieved from multiple sources, the missing figures were calculated based on the average of the two previous years. In the case of Saudi Arabia, the latest available figure (2006) was used as an approximation. For this reason, in some graphs, Saudi Arabia was ommitted.
